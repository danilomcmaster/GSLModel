\begin{abstract}
In this paper, we develop a differential equation to model the dynamics of the Great Salt Lake. We analyze how the dynamics change as the equation changes; for example, how adding salinity to the evaporation rate changes the predictions of our model. 
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The Great Salt Lake (GSL) is of great ecological importance to Utah's environment. In recent years, its footprint has been shrinking. Understanding the dynamics of the lake, specifically the rate at which it is shrinking and what it would take to reverse that shrinking, will allow researchers and citizens of Utah to understand how much time the Great Salt Lake has left. In this paper, we design and analyze a simplified model to understand the dynamics of the Great Salt Lake using an Ordinary Differential Equation. The differential equation consists of an inflow function and an outflow function. This project investigates how changes in the inflow and outflow of the Lake will affect its overall volume, including the effect of human usage. 

Lake dynamics and the GSL specifically have been the subject of research by ecologists for the better part of a century. State-of-the-art models of the Great Salt Lake separate the lake into its North and South arms and model the South arm as two distinct layers: the Upper Brine Layer and a saltier Deep Brine Layer that form due to the higher density of hypersaline water. Water exchange between the arms of the lake is understood to occur at multiple bays, with individual rates that depend on the elevation of the lake and salinity gradients between the arms of the lake (Dunn 2025 et al) \cite{Dunn2025}. In this model, we treated the lake as a homogeneous body of water, aggregating the salinities of the two arms of the lake by averaging them over surface area. While this is unconventional, it simplified the problem significantly. This choice allowed us to focus on modeling other features in more detail. 

Our goal in modeling was to investigate the simplest possible model that would still be able to capture the inherent nature of the system. According to sensitivity analysis of the system by Mohammed et al., the volume of the lake is most sensitive to changes in inflow from streams and evaporation rate (Mohammed and Tarboton, 2012) \cite{Mohammed2012}. Note that inflow from streams is significantly impacted by water diversion for farming and other human usage, so this is the primary control mechanism available to legislation that aims to preserve the lake. Mohammed et al. \cite{Mohammed2012} also found that the variables with the most significant stabilizing effects on water volume are lake surface area and salinity. Both of these variables lead to decreased evaporation when the lake is at a low volume and increased evaporation when the lake is at a high volume, mitigating extreme changes in lake volume. The results of this paper motivated the primary variables we focused on in our model: inflow, evaporation, and salinity. We calculated evaporation rate using a modified Penman equation to account for the effect of salinity on vapor pressure (standard practice in lake dynamics, see Mohammed et. al, Dunn et al.) \cite{Mohammed2012}\cite{Dunn2025}, which required selecting functions for humidity and temperature. We also included a term for precipitation to increase the realism of our model and better explain the historical change in lake volume. 

The function describing the historical volume of the lake can be most accurately recreated using actual data for precipitation, humidity, and temperature, because this method accounts for the variation of the lake due to unpredictable weather effects. However, this method is unable to produce projections for the future of the lake, because exact data for future temperature and precipitation are unknown. Some papers address this using Monte Carlo methods, where possible weather outcomes are simulated using thousands of random draws of distributions that can be derived from historical data (Tarboton 2026 et al) \cite{Tarboton2025}. Such simulations allow researchers to report credible intervals of simulation results for lake volume. In this project, for the sake of simplicity, we opted for a deterministic method where the future of the lake is simulated using interpolated periodic functions for weather variables. Thus our simulations should be interpreted as point estimates for the progression of the lake from some initial conditions assuming it experiences "average" seasonal precipitation, temperature, and humidity. 


\section{Modeling}

We derived a model to describe the change in volume of the Great Salt Lake over time. The simple version of this model incorporates a simple function capturing all of the inflow ($I(t)$) as well as a simple function approximating the evaporation rate ($E(t)$) of the lake proportional to its surface area at time $t$ (we note that the surface area is entirely dependent on the volume of the lake and has no inherent relationship to time). For the most simple system that we will be analyzing we chose not to consider factors such as precipitation, salinity, wind, and other factors. The ODE that represents this relationship is

For each of these component variables, we collected real world data on which to define our differential equation. Temperature, Humidity, Precipitation Depth, and Wind Speed were all gathered from \href{https://www.ncei.noaa.gov/access/search/data-search/global-summary-of-the-day}{National Centers for Environmental Information}. We extracted the data from data stations at Eagle Range on the west side of the lake, the Salt Lake International Airport on the south side of the lake, and Ogden on the east side of the lake. We averaged the data to approximate the values of these variables around the lake. We then defined functions we used to solve the system in the following ways:

\begin{enumerate}
\item Interpolated functions - These use the exact values of the empirical data, and connect each point with a line. These were primarily used to compare the trajectory of our ODE with historical volume data given an initial value.
\item Fitted functions - With the exception of salinity, we approximated these functions using periodic functions fitted to the empirical data using scipy.optimize.curve\_fit (we did also use a fourier transform to fit a temperature function, but this was not used for any of the predictions). Salinity was fit to a function of the form $C_1V^{-1} + C_2V^{-1/3}$ also using the curve\_fit function. The equations for these fitted functions appear below and a plot of their behavior on a one-year interval also appears below in Figure~\ref{fig:autonomous} and Figure ~\ref{fig:nonautonomous}. 
\item Monte Carlo simulation - Precipitation and inflow exhibited random behavior with large variance that was difficult to capture with a simple function. We used a Monte Carlo simulation to be able to simulate this behavior and achieve more accurate prediction.
\end{enumerate}

    \dfrac{dV}{dt} &= I_s(t) + [P_d(V, t) - E_d(V, t)]A(V) \\

\end{equation}

This is a non-autonomous ODE, whose order can depend on different factors of the shape of the lake basin. It is interesting to note that when modeling a shallow lake such as the Great Salt Lake, it is common to approximate the function of surface area as a function of volume as some function resembling $A = kV^a$ for some $a$ usually less than one. We have chosen, however, to represent the function describing surface area in terms of volume as an approximation of real-life bathymetry data. While this choice will help us to construct a closer approximation to the volume data of the Great Salt Lake, it is worth noting that this decision also makes it slightly more difficult to analyze the ODE explicitly since there is no closed form representation of that equation. 

\subsection{Inflow}
There are three main tributaries that flow into the Great Salt Lake. For this model, 
we decided that the inflow, $I(t)$, is a sum of the volume passing from each tributary, recorded daily near the mouth of each from 1998 to the present, and of precipitation multiplied by the surface area of the lake.
To derive a single, analytical function that can model the inflow from rivers, we noted its periodic nature, and made the decision to model it as a sum of two sinusoidal functions, and use scipy.optimize.curve\_fit to fit amplitude, frequency, and phase parameters. We similarly fit a sinusoidal function to precipitation data recorded at two different weather stations around the lake.


Accepting that a decrease in flow from the tributaries is a major factor in the shrinking of the Great Salt Lake,
we investigated how water diversion and basin depletion have affected the inflow of the lake.
Unfortunately we were able to find any quantitative answers that suggested a certain function to model the decrease in inflow,
so we decided to incorporate a simple linear decrease to the inflow function to model increased tributary depletion over the two decades.
The graph of inflow data and the fitted function are shown to the left in Figure~\ref{fig:inflow}.


Unfortunately, modeling the inflow from the tributary rivers as purely periodic does not capture its true behavior.
Random spikes in inflow from the rivers can be better predicted by monte carlo simulations which attempt to simulate a similar sort of random process, thus making predicted results resemble actual expected inflows much more closely. The resulting approximation can be seen on the right of Figure~\ref{fig:inflow}

\subsection{Outflow}
While we initially considered evaporation as a constant, the primary limitation of a constant-rate model is its inability to account for the periodic fluctuations observed in historical lake data. To bridge this gap, we transitioned to a mass transfer model based on Dalton’s Law. This decision stems from the physical reality that evaporation is not a static loss but a dynamic process driven by the vapor pressure deficit which is the difference between the moisture the air can hold and the moisture currently present.

By grounding our model in Dalton’s Law, we define the evaporation rate ($E$) as a function of environmental drivers:

\begin{equation}
    E = f(u) \cdot (e_s - e_a)
\end{equation}
Where $f(u)$ represents a wind-dependent function that models the removal of saturated air from the lake surface. $(e_s - e_a)$ represents the atmospheric pull on lake water, dictated by temperature and humidity.

To capture the impact of seasonal cycles, we treat the evaporation rate as a time-dependent variable within our Ordinary Differential Equation. The resulting model accounts for the downward multi-year trend while allowing for the temporary recovery phases observed during spring runoffs.

To approximate these meteorological drivers, we utilize a sine-wave function for the evaporation coefficient. By incorporating this periodic function, the model becomes responsive to time. While this mass transfer framework is a significant mechanical upgrade from a constant-rate assumption, its current iteration relies on simulated weather parameters rather than historical climate datasets.

\subsection{Salinity}
The GSL is a hyper-saline basin lake, and higher salinity water has a lower saturation vapor pressure, leading to lower evaporation rates. The effect of salinity on evaporation is not linear or logarithmic, but the effect of salinity on saturation vapor pressure is well documented, and reference tables are available. Evaporation rate is generally linear with respect to the deficit between saturation vapor pressure and actual vapor pressure above the surface of the water. Vapor pressure above the surface of the lake can be estimated using dew point or temperature data from nearby weather stations, possibly using monthly averages for computational efficiency. 


To capture the way salinity of the water at the surface changes with volume, we can look at empirical measurements and interpolate between them. Salinity data from the North Arm of the lake (which has much higher salinity) have been averaged over surface area with salinity data from the Upper Brine Layer of the South Arm of the lake, because for this simplified model we are not considering volume of the two halves to be separate.

\subsubsection{Model 2: Weighted average of salinity}

In our next model, we decided to include salinity; see $S(V)$ in Equation 1 for the equation for salinity. The effect of salinity on evaporation is described by the following modified version of our equation for $E_d(V, t)$:

\begin{equation}
    E_d(V, t) = \max[(0.001 + 0.0005W(t)]0.611 * e^{\frac{17.27T(t)}{T(t) + 237.3}}[1 - r_S(S(V)) - H(t)], 0)  \\
\end{equation} 

Where $r_s(c)$ is the percent reduction in vapor pressure (compared to the vapor pressure of freshwater at the same temperature) as a function of salinity. This function is unique to the Great Salt Lake's exact makeup of salts. Raoult's Law is an equation for reduction in vapor pressure as a function of salinity, but it's only accurate for relatively low concentrations of salt. Thus the reduction in vapor pressure in the hypersaline water of the GSL cannot be predicted by Raoult's Law. Dickson et al. \cite{Dickson1965} reports on the ratio for water samples from the GSL at various brine concentrations calculated empirically. This data was limited in the range of brine concentrations tested, so we needed to extend the data into a function. We fit a logistic function to the residuals of the data after subtracting the prediction from Raoult's Law, which resulted in the model shown in Figure~\ref{fig:vapor_pressure_reduc}.

\subsubsection{Model 3: Two halves salinity}
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
    S_s(V) &= 1564600000000V^{-1} - 4334^{-\frac{1}{3}} \\
    S_n(V) &= 662925000000V^{-1} + 462860^{-\frac{1}{3}} \\
    E_d(V, t) &= \max[(0.001 + 0.0005W(t)]0.611 * e^{\frac{17.27T(t)}{T(t) + 237.3}}[1 - r_S^{*}(S_n(V), S_s(V)) - H(t)], 0)  \\
    r_S^{*}(c_n, c_s) &= [K*r_S(c_n) + (1 - K)*r_S(c_s)] \\
    K &= 0.615 \\
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}

For model 3, we decided to model the salinity of the two halves of the lake separately. In model 2, we calculated just one estimate of salinity and used it to get percent reduction in vapor pressure. In this model, we calculated two salinity values: one for the southern part of the lake, $S_s(V)$, and one for the northern part of the lake, $S_n(V)$. We then took a weighted average (weight $K$ is the average ratio of surface area of the north arm of the lake to the surface area of the south arm) of the percent reduction in vapor pressure of these two salinity's to use in our evaporation equation. See Equation 4 for these equations.

The results of this change are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:model_3}. This change to evaporation caused our volume predictions, both with fitted inflow and empirical inflow, to follow the historical volume more closely.

\section{Results}
Using historical inflow data, model 3 performs well, qualitatively, at predicting the volume of the lake. This allows us to conclude that solutions to our differential equation, when given correct values for its input functions, does well at predicting the volume of the lake. However, this model does not lend itself well to future predictions because of its reliance on historical data. This can be seen in model 3’s performance where, when using fitted periodic functions, it follows the overall trend of the data but doesn’t include any of the shocks normally seen in inflow functions. 

Model 5 incorporates Monte Carlo sampling to better represent the unpredictable spikes in inflow. While this stochastic approach does not follow historical data as closely as deterministic models due to its inherent randomness, it is the most appropriate method for future projections where exact inflow values are unavailable.

As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:model_5}, we defined volume thresholds correlating to the ecological health described in \textit{Great Salt Lake Data and Insights Summary} \cite{GSLStrikeTeam2026}. Our simulation results indicate that if no action is taken, or if water usage is restricted by only 250 KAF/year, the probability of reaching a healthy volume by 2036 is negligible. However, if water usage is restricted by 800 KAF/year, there is a 32\% probability that the GSL will reach a healthy level by 2036.


\section{Analysis/Conclusions}

By modeling the Great Salt Lake as a closed-basin system using a first-order ODE, we created a computationally efficient tool that prioritizes key environmental drivers like salinity-dependent evaporation. This approach uses a single state variable ($V$) to follow the mass balance principle, allowing us to focus on the self-stabilizing physics where rising salinity slows evaporation. We utilized a multi-tiered strategy—combining interpolated functions for history, periodic functions for stability analysis, and Monte Carlo simulations for weather variance—to ensure the model was robust enough to handle Utah’s unpredictable climate. Starting with a simple freshwater baseline allowed us to isolate the impacts of salinity and bathymetry, proving that strategic simplifications are essential for verifying complex non-autonomous variables.

Our findings reveal that while the lake shows seasonal fluctuations, it faces a long-term downward trend that requires significant policy intervention. We identified an equilibrium threshold showing that inflow must substantially exceed evaporation for a sustained period to recover the current volume deficit. This suggests that legislative efforts, such as restricting upstream water rights, must be implemented as permanent shifts in water management rather than temporary fixes. Without a long-term increase in basal inflow, any stabilization will be short-lived, and the lake will inevitably return to a state of decline once water diversion resumes.
