\begin{abstract}
In this paper, we develop a differential equation to model the dynamics of the Great Salt Lake. We analyze how the dynamics change as the equation changes; for example, how adding salinity to the evaporation rate changes the predictions of our model. 
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
The Great Salt Lake (GSL) is of great ecological importance to Utah's environment. In recent years, its footprint has been shrinking. Understanding the dynamics of the lake, specifically the rate at which it is shrinking and what it would take to reverse that shrinking, will allow researchers and citizens of Utah to understand how much time the Great Salt Lake has left. In this paper, we design and analyze a simplified model to understand the dynamics of the Great Salt Lake using an Ordinary Differential Equation. The differential equation is comprised of an inflow function and an outflow function. This project investigates how changes in the inflow and outflow of the Lake will affect its overall volume, including the effect of human usage. 

Lake dynamics and the GSL specifically have been the subject of research by ecologists for the better part of a century. State-of-the-art models of the Great Salt Lake separate the lake into its North and South arms and modeling the South arm as two distinct layers: the Upper Brine Layer and a saltier Deep Brine Layer that form due to the higher density of hypersaline water. Water exchange between the arms of the lake is understood to occur at multiple bays, with individual rates that depend on the elevation of the lake and salinity gradients between the arms of the lake (Dunn 2025 et al). In this model, we treat the lake as a homogeneous body of water, aggregating the salinities of the two arms of the lake by averaging them over surface area. While this is unconventional, it simplifies the problem significantly, allowing us to focus on modeling other features in more detail. 

Our goal in modeling was to investigate the simplest possible model that is still able to capture the inherent nature of the system. According to sensitivity analysis of the system by Mohammed et al., the volume of the lake is most sensitive to changes in inflow from streams and evaporation rate (Mohammed and Tarboton, 2012). Note that inflow from streams is significantly impacted by water diversion for farming and other human usage, so this is the primary control mechanism available to legislation that aims to preserve the lake. Mohammed et all. also found that the variables with the most significant stabilizing effects on water volume are lake surface area and salinity. Both of these variables lead to decreased evaporation when the lake is at a low volume and increased evaporation when the lake is at a high volume, mitigating extreme changes in lake volume. The results of this paper motivate the primary variables we focus on modeling and analyzing, which are: inflow, evaporation, and salinity. We calculate evaporation rate while accounting for the effect of salinity required using a modified Penman equation (standard practice in lake dynamics, see Mohommed et. al, Dunn et al.), which requires choosing functions for humidity and temperature. We also include a term for precipitation to increase the realism of our model and better explain the historical change in lake volume. 

The function describing the historical volume of the lake can be most accurately recreated using actual data for precipitation, humidity, and temperature, because this method accounts for the variation of the lake due to unpredictable weather effects. However, this method is unable to produce projections for the future of the lake, because exact data for future temperature and precipitation are unknown. Some papers address this using Monte Carlo methods, where possible weather outcomes are simulated using thousands of random draws of distributions that can be derived from historical data (Tarboton 2026 et al). Such simulations allow researchers to report credible intervals of simulation results for lake volume. In this project, for the sake of simplicity, we opted for a deterministic method where the future of the lake is simulated using interpolated periodic functions for weather variables. Thus our simulations should be interpreted as point estimates for the progression of the lake from some initial conditions assuming it experiences "average" seasonal precipitation, temperature, and humidity.

\subsection{Inflow}
There are three main tributaries that flow into the Great Salt Lake. For this model, 
we decide for the inflow $I(t)$ to be a sum of the volume passing from each tributary, recorded daily near the mouth of each from 1998 to the present.
To derive a single, analytical function that can model this data, we noted its periodic nature, and made the decision to model it as a sum of two sinusoidal functions, and use scipy.optimize.curve\_fit to fit amplitude, frequency, and phase parameters.


Accepting that a decrease in flow from the tributaries is a major factor in the shrinking of the Great Salt Lake,
we investigated how water diversion and basin depletion have affected the inflow of the lake.

\subsection{Outflow}
While we initially considered evaporation as a constant, the underlying data shows clear periodic fluctuations which we concluded were due to seasons. To account for the seasonality of temperature and humidity, we intend to model the evaporation coefficient using a sine function.
\begin{equation}
    k(t) = A \cdot \sin\left(\frac{2\pi}{12}t + \phi\right) + C
\end{equation}
This allows us to simulate the peak evaporation of the hot summer months and the reduced loss during winter. Similarly, this periodic approach helps explain why the lake volume follows a downward trend, consistently losing ground over years, but showing temporary recovery during the spring runoff.

\subsection{Salinity}
The GSL is a hyper-saline basin lake, and higher salinity water has a lower saturation vapor pressure, leading to lower evaporation rates. The effect of salinity on evaporation is not linear or logarithmic, but the effect of salinity on saturation vapor pressure is well documented, and reference tables are available. Evaporation rate is generally linear with respect to the deficit between saturation vapor pressure and actual vapor pressure above the surface of the water. Vapor pressure above the surface of the lake can be estimated using dew point or temperature data from nearby weather stations, possibly using monthly averages for computational efficiency. 

\newline

To capture the way salinity of the water at the surface changes with volume, we can look at empirical measurements and interpolate between them. Salinity data from the North Arm of the lake (which has much higher salinity) have been averaged over surface area with salinity data from the Upper Brine Layer of the South Arm of the lake, because for this simplified model we are not considering volume of the two halves to be separate.

\subsection{Nondimensionalization}

We begin our nondimensionalization by introducing the following constants: 
\begin{itemize}
\item $V_0$: The typical volume of the lake
\item $A_0$: The typical area of the lake
\item $I_0$: The typical inflow of the lake
\item $E_0$: The typical evaporation rate of the lake.
\end{itemize}

We also define new, dimensionless variables $v = \frac{V}{V_0}$ and $\tau = \frac{t}{T}$. With that, we have the conversion $V = vV_0$ and $t = \tau T$. Substituting these variables into our ODE, we have $\frac{V_0}{T}\frac{dv}{d\tau} = I(\tau T) - E(\tau T)A(vV_0)$. We will define a new surface area function $a(v) = \frac{A(vV_0)}{A_0}$. We substitute this into the equation and divide both sides by $I_0$ to get $\frac{V_0}{I_0T}\frac{dv}{d\tau} = \frac{I(\tau T)}{I_0} - \frac{E(\tau T)A_0}{I_0}a(v)$. Setting $T = \frac{V_0}{I_0}$ and defining a new parameter $\gamma = \frac{A_0E_0}{I_0}$, the nondimensionalized equation becomes \[
\frac{dv}{d\tau} = \frac{I(t)}{I_0} - \gamma\frac{E(t)}{E_0}a(v)
\]

\subsection{Equilibria}

Assuming typical or average inflow and evaporation rate, $I_0$ and $E_0$ respectively, our new nondimensionalized system becomes $\frac{dv}{d\tau} = 1 - \gamma a(v)$. So, our equilibria occur where $a(v) = \frac{1}{\gamma} = \frac{I_0}{A_0E_0}$. What this tells us, is that assuming a constant inflow and evaporation rate, around the typical rates we would have an equilibrium point around whichever value for volume gives an area of $a(v) = \frac{1}{\gamma}$.

\subsection{Stability Analysis}

Since we have an equilibrium for $v*$ where $a(v*) = \frac{1}{\gamma}$, we linearize the system around that equilibrium point to determine its stability. Taking $\frac{dv}{d\tau} = f(v) = 1 - \gamma a(v)$, we have $f'(v) = -\gamma a'(v)$. So, we have two cases: i) if $a'(v) > 0$, then $f'(v) < 0$ and the equilibrium is stable or ii) if $a'(v) < 0$, then $f'(v) > 0$ and the equilibrium is unstable. It is reasonable to assume that $a'(v) > 0$ since surface area should increase as volume increases.
