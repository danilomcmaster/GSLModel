\section{Introduction}
The Great Salt Lake is of great ecological importance to Utah's environment. In recent years, its footprint has been shrinking. Understanding the dynamics of the lake, specifically the rate at which it is shrinking and what it would take to reverse that shrinking, will allow researchers and citizens of Utah to understand how much time the Great Salt Lake has left. In this paper, we design and analyze a differential equation to understand the dynamics of the Great Salt Lake. The differential equation is comprised of an inflow function and an outflow function and we consider how changes in the inflow and outflow of the Lake will affect its overall volume. 

\section{Modeling}

We derived a model to describe the change in volume of the Great Salt Lake over time. The simple version of this model incorporates a simple function capturing all of the inflow ($I(t)$) as well as a simple function approximating the evaporation rate ($E(t)$) of the lake proportional to its surface area at time $t$ (we note that the surface area is entirely dependent on the volume of the lake and has no inherent relationship to time). For the most simple system that we will be analyzing we chose not to consider factors such as precipitation, salinity, wind, and other factors. The ODE that represents this relationship is
\begin{equation}
    \frac{dV}{dt} = I(t) - E(t)A(V).
\end{equation}
This is a nonautonomous ODE, whose order can depend on different factors of the shape of the lake basin. It is interesting to note that when modeling a shallow lake such as the Great Salt Lake, it is common to approximate the function of surface area as a function of volume as some function resembling $A = kV^a$ for some $a$ usually less than one. We have chosen, however, to represent the function describing surface area in terms of volume as an approximation of real-life bathymetry data. While this choice will help us to construct a closer approximation to the volume data of the Great Salt Lake, it is worth noting that this decision also makes it slightly more difficult to analyze the ODE explicitly since there is no closed form representation of that equation.

\subsection{Outflow}
While we initially considered evaporation as a constant, the underlying data shows clear periodic fluctuations which we concluded were due to seasons. To account for the seasonality of temperature and humidity, we intend to model the evaporation coefficient using a sine function.
\begin{equation}
    k(t) = A \cdot \sin\left(\frac{2\pi}{12}t + \phi\right) + C
\end{equation}
This allows us to simulate the peak evaporation of the hot summer months and the reduced loss during winter. Similarly, this periodic approach helps explain why the lake volume follows a downward trend, consistently losing ground over years, but showing temporary recovery during the spring runoff.

\subsection{Salinity}
The GSL is a hyper-saline basin lake, and higher salinity water has a lower saturation vapor pressure, leading to lower evaporation rates. The effect of salinity on evaporation is not linear or logarithmic, but the effect of salinity on saturation vapor pressure is well documented, and reference tables are available. Evaporation rate is generally linear with respect to the deficit between saturation vapor pressure and actual vapor pressure above the surface of the water. Vapor pressure above the surface of the lake can be estimated using dew point or temperature data from nearby weather stations, possibly using monthly averages for computational efficiency. 

\newline

To capture the way salinity of the water at the surface changes with volume, we can look at empirical measurements and interpolate between them. Salinity data from the North Arm of the lake (which has much higher salinity) have been averaged over surface area with salinity data from the Upper Brine Layer of the South Arm of the lake, because for this simplified model we are not considering volume of the two halves to be separate.

\subsection{Nondimensionalization}

We begin our nondimensionalization by introducing the following constants: 
\begin{itemize}
\item $V_0$: The typical volume of the lake
\item $A_0$: The typical area of the lake
\item $I_0$: The typical inflow of the lake
\item $E_0$: The typical evaporation rate of the lake.
\end{itemize}

We also define new, dimensionless variables $v = \frac{V}{V_0}$ and $\tau = \frac{t}{T}$. With that, we have the conversion $V = vV_0$ and $t = \tau T$. Substituting these variables into our ODE, we have $\frac{V_0}{T}\frac{dv}{d\tau} = I(\tau T) - E(\tau T)A(vV_0)$. We will define a new surface area function $a(v) = \frac{A(vV_0)}{A_0}$. We substitute this into the equation and divide both sides by $I_0$ to get $\frac{V_0}{I_0T}\frac{dv}{d\tau} = \frac{I(\tau T)}{I_0} - \frac{E(\tau T)A_0}{I_0}a(v)$. Setting $T = \frac{V_0}{I_0}$ and defining a new parameter $\gamma = \frac{A_0E_0}{I_0}$, the nondimensionalized equation becomes \[
\frac{dv}{d\tau} = \frac{I(t)}{I_0} - \gamma\frac{E(t)}{E_0}a(v)
\]

\subsection{Equilibria}

Assuming typical or average inflow and evaporation rate, $I_0$ and $E_0$ respectively, our new nondimensionalized system becomes $\frac{dv}{d\tau} = 1 - \gamma a(v)$. So, our equilibria occur where $a(v) = \frac{1}{\gamma} = \frac{I_0}{A_0E_0}$. What this tells us, is that assuming a constant inflow and evaporation rate, around the typical rates we would have an equilibrium point around whichever value for volume gives an area of $a(v) = \frac{1}{\gamma}$.
